No. Inhesion is not commutative, not symmetrical, and not reciprocal in the strict same-relation sense. It is an asymmetric, directional, categorially ordered relation between a dependent determination and its bearer.

The basic form is:

    D inheres in S

where:

    D = dependent determination: property, quality, accident, mode, state, disposition, feature
    S = bearer: subject, substance, substrate, thing qualified, entity determined

Example:

    redness inheres in the apple

This does not imply:

    the apple inheres in redness

The reverse is not merely false; in the classical substance/accident framework it is a category mistake. Redness is the dependent determination. The apple is the bearer. Their roles are not interchangeable.

The correct converse is not:

    S inheres in D

but:

    S bears D
    S has D
    S is qualified by D
    S is determined by D

So the proper pair is:

    redness inheres in the apple
    the apple bears redness

not:

    redness inheres in the apple
    the apple inheres in redness

I. Strict answer

Inhesion has this logical profile:

| Question | Answer | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Is it commutative? | No, if “commutative” means “the terms can be swapped” | The order D → S is essential |
| Is it symmetrical? | No | If D inheres in S, S does not inhere in D |
| Is it reciprocal? | Not in the strict same-predicate sense | The reverse relation is “bears,” not “inheres in” |
| Is it directional? | Yes | It runs from dependent determination to bearer |
| Is it asymmetric? | Yes | The relation cannot be reversed while preserving meaning |
| Does it have a converse? | Yes | “D inheres in S” corresponds to “S bears D” |
| Is it a relation of dependence? | Yes | D depends on S for concrete existence |
| Is it a relation of qualification? | Yes | S is made to be thus-and-so by D |

The compact answer is:

    InheresIn(D, S) is true.
    InheresIn(S, D) is false or categorially improper.
    Bears(S, D) is true.

Therefore:

    InheresIn(D, S) ⇔ Bears(S, D)

but not:

    InheresIn(D, S) ⇔ InheresIn(S, D)

II. Clarifying “commutative”

Strictly speaking, “commutative” is primarily a property of operations, not relations.

For example, addition is commutative:

    a + b = b + a

Multiplication is also commutative over ordinary numbers:

    a × b = b × a

But subtraction is not commutative:

    a - b ≠ b - a

Relations are more commonly classified as symmetrical, asymmetrical, reflexive, transitive, and so on.

So the technically precise question is not:

    Is inhesion commutative?

but:

    Is inhesion symmetrical?
    Can the relata be reversed?
    Is the converse relation the same as the original relation?

The answer is no.

However, if by “commutative” you mean “can I swap the terms without changing the truth or structure?”, then inhesion is not commutative.

Why?

Because:

    redness inheres in the apple

does not commute into:

    the apple inheres in redness

The order is essential.

The structure is:

    dependent determination → bearer

not:

    bearer → dependent determination

III. Clarifying “symmetrical”

A relation R is symmetrical when:

    R(a, b) → R(b, a)

Example:

    a is equal in height to b
    therefore b is equal in height to a

or:

    a is a sibling of b
    therefore b is a sibling of a

But inhesion is not like this.

If:

    fragility inheres in glass

it does not follow that:

    glass inheres in fragility

If:

    heat inheres in iron

it does not follow that:

    iron inheres in heat

If:

    curvature inheres in a surface

it does not follow that:

    the surface inheres in curvature

If:

    courage inheres in a person

it does not follow that:

    the person inheres in courage

So inhesion is not symmetrical.

Its formal structure is:

    I(D, S) → not I(S, D)

where:

    I = inheres in
    D = dependent determination
    S = bearer

This makes it asymmetrical, at least under the standard categorial interpretation.

IV. Clarifying “reciprocal”

“Reciprocal” is more ambiguous than “symmetrical.” It can mean several things.

1. Same-relation reciprocity

This would mean:

    if D inheres in S,
    then S inheres in D.

Inhesion is not reciprocal in this sense.

The apple does not inhere in its redness.
The glass does not inhere in its fragility.
The person does not inhere in courage.
The surface does not inhere in curvature.

2. Converse-relation reciprocity

This means that the original relation has a corresponding inverse expression.

In this weaker sense, inhesion has a converse.

If:

    D inheres in S

then:

    S bears D

So:

    redness inheres in the apple
    the apple bears redness

    fragility inheres in the glass
    the glass bears fragility

    heat inheres in the iron
    the iron bears heat

    courage inheres in the person
    the person bears courage

This is a reciprocal-looking pair, but it is not symmetry. It is converse correlation.

The relation has two sides:

    inhering side: D inheres in S
    bearing side: S bears D

But the two sides are not the same predicate.

The relation is therefore correlative, not symmetrical.

3. Mutual-dependence reciprocity

Another possible meaning of reciprocal is mutual dependence.

Inhesion is not reciprocal in this strong ontological sense either.

The dependent determination depends on the bearer for its concrete existence.

For example:

    this apple's redness depends on this apple as its bearer

But the apple does not usually depend on this exact redness in the same way. The apple may become green, brown, bruised, pale, or rotten while remaining the same apple for some period of change.

So the dependence is not equal.

The determination depends on the bearer for being.
The bearer is qualified by the determination, but is not usually existentially dependent on that exact determination.

V. The precise logical structure

Let:

    I(x, y) = x inheres in y
    B(y, x) = y bears x

Then:

    I(D, S) ⇔ B(S, D)

This means:

    D inheres in S if and only if S bears D.

But:

    I(D, S) does not imply I(S, D)

Indeed, under the standard meaning:

    I(D, S) → not I(S, D)

So:

    I ≠ I⁻¹

That is:

    the inheres-in relation is not identical with its converse.

Its converse is:

    is borne by
    is had by
    is possessed as a determination by
    is predicable of
    qualifies

Thus:

    InheresIn(redness, apple)
    Bears(apple, redness)

not:

    InheresIn(apple, redness)

VI. Why the reversal fails

The reversal fails because inhesion is not a relation between two terms of the same ontological rank.

It is not like:

    sibling of
    adjacent to
    equal to
    similar to

Those relations can often hold between terms of the same type.

Inhesion relates terms of different roles:

    accident → substance
    quality → qualified thing
    mode → modal subject
    state → state-bearer
    property-instance → propertied individual
    disposition → disposed object
    attribute → attributed entity

The relation is therefore categorially ordered.

The inhering term is the one whose being is dependent.
The bearer is the one in which, of which, or through which the dependent determination exists.

So:

    D inheres in S

means:

    D exists as a determination of S.

But:

    S inheres in D

would mean:

    S exists as a determination of D.

That reverses the ontological order.

For example:

    redness exists as a determination of the apple

is intelligible.

But:

    the apple exists as a determination of redness

is not the same kind of claim. It would treat the apple as if it were an accident, mode, or property of redness.

That is why the reversal is not valid.

VII. Inhesion as asymmetric dependence

The core of inhesion is dependence.

The inhering determination depends on the bearer.

Examples:

    this wrinkle depends on this cloth
    this curve depends on this line or surface
    this smile depends on this face or expressive body
    this heat depends on this heated body
    this velocity depends on this moving object
    this state depends on this system
    this habit depends on this person
    this attribute depends on this object

In each case, the first term does not stand alone in the same way as the bearer.

The wrinkle is a wrinkle of the cloth.
The smile is a smile of the face.
The velocity is the velocity of the moving body.
The habit is the habit of the person.
The attribute is the attribute of the object.

This “of” marks dependent belonging.

So the asymmetry is:

    dependent belongs to bearer

not:

    bearer belongs to dependent in the same way

VIII. Relation to predication

Inhesion also grounds predication.

If:

    redness inheres in the apple

then we can say:

    the apple is red

If:

    fragility inheres in the glass

then we can say:

    the glass is fragile

If:

    heat inheres in the iron

then we can say:

    the iron is hot

If:

    courage inheres in the person

then we can say:

    the person is courageous

The grammatical direction is usually:

    subject → predicate

But the ontological direction of inhesion is:

    predicate-determination → subject-bearer

So the two directions are converse perspectives on the same structure.

Grammatical predication:

    S is D

Ontological inhesion:

    D inheres in S

Converse bearing:

    S bears D

Example:

    The apple is red.
    Redness inheres in the apple.
    The apple bears redness.

These are not three symmetrical relations. They are three different ways of articulating one ordered structure.

IX. Relation to “having”

The same applies to “having.”

We may say:

    the apple has redness
    the glass has fragility
    the person has courage
    the object has mass
    the system has instability

But this “having” is not external possession.

It is not like:

    the person has a book
    the house has a garden
    the car has a passenger

Inhesive having means:

    the subject is qualified by a determination

So:

    S has D

means:

    D inheres in S

But again, this is a converse expression, not symmetry.

X. Comparison with genuinely symmetrical relations

To see the contrast, compare inhesion with symmetrical relations.

1. Siblinghood

    Alice is a sibling of Bruno.
    Bruno is a sibling of Alice.

This is symmetrical.

2. Equality

    A is equal to B.
    B is equal to A.

This is symmetrical.

3. Similarity

    A is similar to B.
    B is similar to A.

This is usually symmetrical, at least in ordinary usage.

4. Inhesion

    Redness inheres in the apple.
    The apple inheres in redness.

This is not symmetrical.

The difference is that siblinghood, equality, and similarity generally relate terms at the same level. Inhesion does not. It relates a dependent determination to its bearer.

XI. Comparison with genuinely reciprocal relations

Some relations are reciprocal because they generate paired roles.

Examples:

    teacher ↔ student
    buyer ↔ seller
    parent ↔ child
    lender ↔ borrower
    speaker ↔ hearer

If A is a buyer from B, B is a seller to A.
If A is a parent of B, B is a child of A.
If A lends to B, B borrows from A.

These are reciprocal because each side has a role that answers the other.

Inhesion has something analogous but weaker:

    inhering determination ↔ bearer

If D inheres in S, S bears D.

So there is a paired role-structure.

But the relation is not reciprocal in the sense that the same predicate applies backward.

The correct pair is:

    inheres in ↔ bears

not:

    inheres in ↔ inheres in

Therefore, inhesion is converse-correlative, not symmetrical-reciprocal.

XII. Relation-theoretic classification

Using standard relation properties:

1. Reflexive?

A relation R is reflexive when:

    R(a, a)

for every a.

Inhesion is not reflexive.

A thing does not normally inhere in itself.

    Redness does not inhere in redness in the same sense.
    The apple does not inhere in the apple.
    Fragility does not inhere in fragility.

So inhesion is not reflexive.

2. Irreflexive?

A relation R is irreflexive when:

    not R(a, a)

for every a.

Inhesion is generally irreflexive under the standard interpretation.

Nothing is its own inhering accident in the same respect.

3. Symmetric?

A relation R is symmetric when:

    R(a, b) → R(b, a)

Inhesion is not symmetric.

4. Asymmetric?

A relation R is asymmetric when:

    R(a, b) → not R(b, a)

Inhesion is asymmetric, provided the terms are being used in their proper categorial roles.

If D inheres in S, S does not inhere in D.

5. Antisymmetric?

A relation R is antisymmetric when:

    R(a, b) and R(b, a) → a = b

Inhesion is better described as asymmetric than merely antisymmetric. Antisymmetry is weaker and is more relevant to order relations such as “less than or equal to.” Inhesion is not just a relation where mutuality collapses identity; mutuality is categorially excluded.

6. Transitive?

A relation R is transitive when:

    R(a, b) and R(b, c) → R(a, c)

Inhesion is not generally transitive.

If a determination of a determination is possible — for example, the intensity of a color — it does not automatically follow that the intensity inheres in the original bearer in exactly the same way as the color does.

Example:

    intensity modifies redness
    redness inheres in the apple

It does not straightforwardly follow, without qualification, that:

    intensity inheres in the apple

in the same direct sense.

The better analysis may be layered:

    intensity inheres in this-redness
    this-redness inheres in the apple
    therefore the apple is red-with-a-certain-intensity

But the relation is mediated, not simply transitive.

XIII. The categorial ladder

Inhesion presupposes a hierarchy:

    bearer
      └── determination
            └── determination-of-determination

Example:

    apple
      └── redness
            └── intensity of redness

or:

    person
      └── courage
            └── firmness of courage

or:

    surface
      └── curvature
            └── degree of curvature

This shows why inhesion is ordered. Lower-level determinations are nested in higher-level bearers or in other determinations, but the direction does not simply reverse.

XIV. Examples across domains

1. Physical quality

    heat inheres in iron
    iron bears heat

Not:

    iron inheres in heat

2. Shape

    roundness inheres in the ball
    the ball bears roundness

Not:

    the ball inheres in roundness

3. Color

    redness inheres in the rose
    the rose bears redness

Not:

    the rose inheres in redness

4. Disposition

    fragility inheres in glass
    glass bears fragility

Not:

    glass inheres in fragility

5. Psychological habit

    patience inheres in the person
    the person bears patience

Not:

    the person inheres in patience

6. Social status

    authority inheres in the office-holder, under an institutional order
    the office-holder bears authority

Not:

    the office-holder inheres in authority

7. Computational analogy

    an attribute inheres in an object
    the object bears the attribute

Not:

    the object inheres in the attribute

8. Linguistic analogy

    tense inheres in a verb form
    the verb form bears tense

Not:

    the verb form inheres in tense

XV. Important qualification

Inhesion should not be confused with mutual constitution.

Some philosophical systems may challenge the classical substance/accident model. For example, bundle theories may say that an object is nothing over and above a bundle of properties. Trope theories may treat property-instances as particularized dependent entities. Process metaphysics may treat stable bearers as derivative from processes.

But even in those alternative frameworks, the relation corresponding to inhesion remains non-symmetric if it preserves the distinction between:

    that which qualifies

and:

    that which is qualified

As long as the relation means:

    D is a determination of S

then it is directional.

If a theory abolishes the distinction between bearer and determination altogether, then it is no longer using “inhesion” in the classical sense.

XVI. Final distilled answer

Inhesion is not commutative, reciprocal, or symmetrical in the strict sense.

It is:

    asymmetric
    directional
    non-commutative in the loose role-swapping sense
    non-symmetric in the strict relational sense
    non-reciprocal under the same predicate
    converse-correlative through the paired relation “bears”
    grounded in ontological dependence
    structured by the distinction between dependent determination and bearer

The final formula is:

    D inheres in S
    ⇔
    S bears D

but:

    D inheres in S
    ⇏
    S inheres in D

Therefore:

    Inhesion(D, S) is not symmetrical.
    Inhesion(D, S) is not reversible.
    Inhesion(D, S) is not reciprocal as Inhesion(S, D).
    Inhesion(D, S) only has a converse: Bearing(S, D).

Most compactly:

    Inhesion is not a relation of mutuality.
    It is a relation of dependent belonging.

Or:

    The property inheres in the bearer;
    the bearer bears the property;
    neither term can replace the other without destroying the relation.
